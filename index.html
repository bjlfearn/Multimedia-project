Paradise under threat?

A look at the Monsal Trail and the Peak District

Story by Ben Fearn

One of the most visited National Parks in the world...

Derbyshire is well known for tourist hotspots such as Chatsworth and Bakewell. When thinking about Derbyshire, the area which may not spring immediately to mind is the Monsal Trail. It's not as well known a monument as Chatsworth House, but it attracts thousands of visitors every year.

When you see pictures of Monsal Head and the Monsal Dale, it’s not difficult to see why it’s so popular. According to the Peak District National Park, up to 30 million people visit the Peaks every year, second only to Mount Fuji as the most visited national park in the world.

Are you one of the 30 million who visits places like this every year?

The Monsal Trail is a traffic free route through some of the Peak District's most spectacular limestone dales.

Originally built in 1863, the viaduct at Monsal Head is 300 feet long and has five 50 foot span arches.

The former Midland Railway line once provided the link between Manchester and London, before closing in 1968.

The route remained closed from 1968 until 1981, with the route expanding further in 2011.

However, with the clear attraction of the area comes a concern for the environmental impact. An estimated 20 million people live within an hour’s drive of the Peak District.

The Peak District National Park outlines two purposes in this regard: “to conserve and enhance the natural beauty, wildlife and cultural heritage of the Peak District”, and “to promote opportunities for the understanding and enjoyment of the Peak District's special qualities”.

John Ruskin, one of Victorian Britain's most influential writers on culture, was less kind about the scenery at Monsal Dale. When the railway line was constructed in 1863, he had the following words to say about it:

'There was a rocky valley between Buxton and Bakewell, once upon a time, divine as the Vale of Tempe... You Enterprised a Railroad through the valley – you blasted its rocks away, heaped thousands of tons of shale into its lovely stream. The valley is gone, and the Gods with it; and now, every fool in Buxton can be in Bakewell in half an hour, and every fool in Bakewell at Buxton; which you think a lucrative process of exchange – you Fools everywhere'.

'You fools everywhere!'

Environmental concerns are not new to the area, it seems. Mr Ruskin would no doubt be pleased with the area as it is now, but whilst the concerns for him were about carving rock in an area of natural beauty, the 21st century challenge is to preserve the Peaks in a time of climate change and population growth.

The Peak District has a proud record of being the first recognised National Park in the UK (1951). Since the Peak District was recognised as a National Park in 1951, the UK population has grown from just over 50 million to over 63 million today.

Another statistic often quoted is that 75% of people in Derbyshire live on 25% of the land. Green and open spaces in Derbyshire are therefore common, but with a housing crisis in most parts of the country, could the Monsal Dale one day give way to housing redevelopments?
